SECTION 01 EXECUTIVE SUMMARY
Angawatch — from the Swahili-Maa fusion of anga (sky/atmosphere) and watch (guardian) — is Kenya’s entry for Power the Community 2026. It is a ground-up community for 2,000 families on the Yatta Plateau, Machakos County, 60km from Nairobi. Angawatch answers the competition’s founding question directly: we do not retrofit solar onto existing houses. We build houses around solar.
What Angawatch Is — And What It Deliberately Is Not
Angawatch is designed for economic optimisation and housing affordability — not off-grid environmental purity. This distinction is deliberate and honest. A nurse earning KES 40,000/month cannot afford the capital cost of a system engineered to function in complete grid isolation. What she can afford is a home where her electricity bill drops from KES 11,500 to KES 930 per month, where her mortgage is KES 33,015, and where her children go to school 400 metres from her front door. Angawatch is a Grid-Interactive Microgrid with Islanding Capability — it connects to the KPLC grid, exports surplus solar for income, imports emergency power during the long rains, and can island-operate during grid outages. This architecture is cheaper, more resilient, and more bankable than a true off-grid system of equivalent size.
Why Biogas Goes to Schools and Clinics — Not Homes
The community biogas plant produces 773 m³/day of usable gas. This is not enough to cook for 2,000 families — and any document that claims otherwise is mathematically incorrect. The correct figure for 2,000 households would require approximately 3,200 m³/day, more than four times what our system produces. The honest engineering decision: direct all institutional biogas to the Community Health Centre, two schools, and the community market kitchen. These three facilities currently cook on LPG or charcoal. Replacing their cooking fuel with biogas eliminates PM2.5 exposure for 400 children in school kitchens daily and reduces institutional cooking costs by KES 890,000/year. This is a higher-impact use of limited biogas than spreading it thinly across 2,000 homes where LPG SACCO bulk-procurement already delivers a 15–20% cost reduction. Individual homes transition from SACCO LPG to solar induction cooking in Phase 3 (Year 10+) as solar surplus grows. Maximum impact with the resource available — not maximum coverage.
The 80/20 Principle — Three Decisions Deliver 80% of All Benefit
In the spirit of the competition’s engineering philosophy — most improvement with least input — three decisions account for 80% of Angawatch’s resident benefit:
DECISION 1 — Biogas system + community LPG SACCO replacing charcoal. The biogas plant (KES 65M) supplies clean gas to the CHC, schools, and community market, eliminating PM2.5 from institutional cooking entirely. Individual households shift from retail LPG (KES 1,560/month) to community SACCO bulk-procured LPG (KES 1,260–1,330/month), saving KES 230–300/month immediately. Phase 3 (Year 10+) transitions homes to solar induction cooking on surplus solar. The biogas system generates 254 MWh/year electricity (with 40-cow commercial dairy). BCR ~2.8 — still the best investment in the project.
DECISION 2 — CSEB construction from Yatta volcanic laterite. Eliminates mechanical cooling entirely (zero kWh for AC), reduces build cost 35% vs concrete block, employs community labour during construction, and provides lifelong passive thermal comfort. Zero operating cost.
DECISION 3 — M-Pesa SmartMeter community tariff at KES 9/kWh. Requires KES 2.5M software development. At 150 kWh/month planned household consumption, saves each family KES 2,400/month versus KPLC’s KES 25/kWh (KES 1,350 vs KES 3,750 for same 150 kWh). A Nairobi-equivalent household consuming 460 kWh/month saves KES 7,360/month — the community tariff rate advantage is 64% regardless of consumption level. Citizens buy their own units, control their own consumption, receive net metering credits directly to their mobile wallet — energy governance controlled by residents, not a central utility.

SECTION 03 CRITICAL NEEDS — STATEMENT SUPPORTED BY REFERENCES
Every design decision in Angawatch traces to a specific, referenced problem. The following six needs are documented from government, WHO, and field data sources. References appear in Section 22.
Need 1 — Household Air Pollution from Solid Fuel Cooking (and why LPG alone is not enough)
The World Health Organisation (2022) reports that household air pollution from cooking on solid fuels — charcoal, firewood, animal dung — is responsible for 3.2 million deaths per year globally, including over 237,000 deaths of children under five. Around 2.4 billion people cook this way. In Machakos County, over 70% of rural households cook on charcoal or firewood (KNBS 2019 Census). Indoor PM2.5 concentrations during charcoal cooking reach 300–1,000 µg/m³ — up to 67× the WHO 24-hour safe limit of 15 µg/m³. Angawatch’s three-phase approach: Phase 1 — all residents switch from charcoal to LPG via community bulk SACCO procurement (immediate PM2.5 reduction). Phase 2 — biogas supplied to CHC, schools, and community market (institutional cooking goes near-zero PM2.5). Phase 3 — solar induction cooking for homes (Year 10+ as solar surplus grows). LPG with proper ventilation is a dramatic improvement over charcoal — PM2.5 drops from 300–1,000 µg/m³ to 10–30 µg/m³, far below charcoal but higher than biogas. This phased approach is honest and achievable.
Need 2 — Energy Poverty
KPLC connection rates in Yatta sub-county: 45–60%, far below Nairobi’s 95%+. Where KPLC reaches homes, domestic tariffs of KES 25–28/kWh consume a disproportionate share of income. The Yatta region experiences 15–25 power outages per year, with each outage averaging 4–8 hours (KPLC Outage Report 2024). Angawatch’s community solar provides power at KES 9/kWh — 64% cheaper — with 99.6% annual uptime.
Need 3 — Cooking Cost and Transition Strategy
Current situation: 70%+ of Yatta households cook on charcoal or firewood. The nurse-teacher family that has already switched to LPG pays KES 1,560/month (1.2 × 6kg cylinders). Those still on charcoal pay KES 2,500–4,000/month but suffer severe indoor air pollution. Angawatch’s phased cooking strategy: Phase 1 (Years 1–5) — Community LPG SACCO buys cylinders in bulk from TOTAL Energies Kenya at 15–20% discount. Resident cost: KES 1,260–1,330/month. Saving vs retail LPG: KES 230–300/month. Saving vs charcoal: KES 1,200–2,700/month. Phase 2 — Biogas from the community plant supplies the CHC, schools, and community market kitchen. Phase 3 (Year 10+) — Solar induction cooking for homes as solar surplus grows. From charcoal to LPG to solar: a realistic, funded, 10-year cooking transition.
Need 4 — Transport Poverty
Current matatu fares: Matuu to Machakos KES 120–180 one way. Return: KES 240–360/day × 22 working days = KES 5,280–7,920/month. Angawatch electric community e-matatu: KES 80–100/trip × 2 × 22 days = KES 3,520–4,400/month. Saving: ~KES 4,840/month. Boda operators currently spend KES 6,000–8,000/month on petrol. Roam Air electric boda: KES 50/day charging = KES 1,500/month. Saving: KES 5,500–6,500/month.
Need 5 — Healthcare Access
Machakos County doctor-to-patient ratio: 1:8,000 (Machakos County CIDP 2023–2027). WHO recommended minimum: 1:1,000. Angawatch’s 50-bed CHC staffed primarily by resident nurse families brings healthcare within walking distance. Telemedicine link to Machakos Level 5 Hospital via broadband connectivity.
Need 6 — Education Infrastructure
Yatta sub-county school pupil-to-classroom ratio: 50+ (national average). Angawatch Primary and Secondary schools achieve 30 pupils per class. With approximately 300 resident teacher families, teachers live within walking distance of their workplace — eliminating the teacher housing shortage that afflicts rural Kenyan schools.
SECTION 04 COMMUNITY ARCHETYPES — WHO ANGAWATCH IS BUILT FOR
To guide our engineering parameters, financial modelling, and unit mix decisions, we developed three Core User Archetypes. These are Human-Centered Design composite personas — not specific individuals quoted verbatim, but representative profiles constructed from KNBS 2019 Census data, Kenya TSC salary scales (2023), NCK nurse grade salary schedules, and brief on-the-ground surveys with residents of Matuu and Yatta townships in early 2026. Every design decision — housing size, transport cost, cooking fuel, energy budget — is calibrated against at least one archetype.
Archetype 1: The Rural Nurse — Composite profile representing public health and education households earning KES 95,000–134,000 combined
This archetype represents the dual-income professional family — the primary beneficiary and most common household type at Angawatch, making up approximately 60% of the 3BR resident population.
What residents in this income bracket actually say when asked about energy costs: ‘Tokens zinaisha haraka sana — na wakati wa load shedding, chakula inaharibika.’ (Tokens run out too fast — and when there’s load shedding, the food goes bad.) This reflects the KNBS finding that 68% of peri-urban Machakos households cite electricity cost and reliability as their top household expense concern after rent.
What they say about cooking: ‘Smoke from the jiko makes the children cough, but gas is too expensive to buy all at once — you cannot buy half a cylinder.’ This captures the real barrier: not the monthly cost but the upfront refill cost of KES 1,300–1,500 in a single transaction. The SACCO bulk procurement model solves this by spreading the cost.
Archetype 2: The Boda Boda Operator — Composite profile representing transport and informal trade workers earning KES 30,000–48,000/month
This archetype represents the self-employed informal sector worker — the 1BR cooperative rental resident. Approximately 7.5% of the Angawatch population. The honest design tension: this income bracket is at the lower edge of affordability even for the 1BR cooperative rental.
What residents in this income bracket actually say: ‘Boda ni kazi ngumu — petrol inapanda kila wiki, lakini hakuna alternative.’ (Boda is hard work — petrol rises every week, but there is no alternative.) This reflects EPRA data showing petrol prices in Machakos County rose 34% between January 2022 and December 2023.
What they say about housing aspiration: ‘Nataka mahali salama — si lazima kubwa, lakini salama na stima inayofanya kazi.’ (I want somewhere safe — not necessarily big, but safe and with electricity that works.) The 1BR cooperative rental at KES 10,000–12,000/month directly addresses this.
Archetype 3: The Deputy Principal — Composite profile representing senior civil servants and upper-tier professionals earning KES 120,000–200,000 combined
This archetype represents the 4BR resident — approximately 1% of the population. A small but important cohort: if Angawatch cannot attract senior professionals, the schools and CHC cannot function at the standard the community requires.
What residents at this income level say about Yatta: ‘The land is good and it is not far, but who will teach my children if all the good teachers are in Nairobi?’ This is the circular problem Angawatch breaks: senior professionals will not move to areas that lack quality schools and medical facilities. The integrated 30-pupil-per-class school and 50-bed CHC are the answer.
What they say about energy: ‘I work night shifts — I need the lights and the fridge to work. I cannot budget for a generator every time KPLC fails.’ The 99.6% uptime argument is more important for this archetype than the cost savings.

SECTION 07 PASSIVE DESIGN &** BUILDING ENGINEERING**
All energy efficiency in the housing units is achieved through passive design — no active mechanical systems. At Yatta Plateau’s elevation and latitude, first-principles analysis shows that passive measures alone are sufficient to maintain comfort across all seasons.
1. Thermal Mass — CSEB Walls
300mm CSEB walls (Yatta basaltic laterite + 5% cement) provide a thermal mass of approximately 450 kJ/m²·K. Yatta’s diurnal temperature swing is 12–14°C (peak 29–32°C afternoon, trough 16–18°C dawn). The CSEB wall absorbs heat during the hot afternoon and releases it slowly overnight, maintaining interior temperatures within 20–26°C throughout the 24-hour cycle. This eliminates all mechanical cooling — the walls are the air conditioner.
2. Ventilated Roof Cavity — NOT Insulation
Wall insulation is NOT specified and would be harmful. Standard insulation (rockwool, foam board) traps heat inside the building at night, preventing the natural cooling that Yatta’s cold nights provide. Instead, a 150mm ventilated air cavity sits between the concrete slab and the corrugated iron roof sheet. Hot air from the roof surface rises through the gap and exits via ridge vents, keeping the slab 8–12°C cooler than the roof surface. This eliminates roof-to-ceiling radiant heat gain during the hottest hours. Heating demand = ZERO. Yatta’s minimum temperature (12°C, July nights) is within the range Kenyan residents find comfortable under bedding — no heating system is installed or budgeted.
3. Yatta Laterite-Lime Render — Local Material Innovation
External wall surfaces use Yatta laterite-lime render: 15mm of on-site Yatta Formation basaltic laterite mixed with 15% hydrated lime and 5% CSEB fines. This is not standard cement plaster. It is breathable (vapour-permeable), allowing the CSEB wall to regulate moisture without condensation. The off-white natural pigment achieves 60% solar reflectance vs 30% for dark cement plaster. Applied by community self-help groups trained during construction — lowers labour cost and transfers skills. Local name: ‘Rangi ya Ardhi’ (Earth Colour).
4. Cross-Ventilation — Louvred Windows
Standard adjustable aluminium louvred windows — NOT casement (casement = side-hinged swinging frame, incompatible with louvres) and NOT double-glazed (cold climate technology, would trap daytime heat). Each apartment has east-facing and west-facing louvred windows: horizontal aluminium slat louvres operated by an internal hand lever. The lever turns the slat angle from fully closed to fully open, providing 3–5 air changes per hour. Externally mounted insect screens (fine mesh, no frame intrusion into lever mechanism) protect against malaria-bearing mosquitoes. Stack ventilation through the open stairwell draws air passively upward through the building.
5. Solar Water Heating — Eliminating Electric Geysers
Every unit has a 2m² flat-plate solar thermal collector on the roof feeding a 150L insulated tank. At 5.18 kWh/m²/day irradiance and 65% thermal efficiency, each system delivers 95–120 litres of hot water per day (sufficient for 4–5 people showering). Electric instant water heaters (Ariston-type commonly used in Kenyan middle-income housing) draw 3–5 kW — their complete elimination saves 1.5 kWh/day per household = approximately 1,095 MWh/year community-wide (2,000 units × 1.5 kWh × 365). This saving is already embedded in the residential appliance audit tables which show 0 kWh for water heating — the solar thermal system is the reason.
6. Underground Cabling — Not Overhead
All internal distribution cables are underground XLPE armoured cable. Overhead line alternatives were considered and rejected: (1) Machakos County experiences severe afternoon thunderstorms March–May — overhead lines suffer disproportionate fault rates during these events. (2) A 90-hectare planned community with 20 Ekeja clusters and regular tree planting would require continuous wayleave clearing for overhead lines. (3) Underground cabling is more aesthetically appropriate for a premium community and eliminates visual clutter over green spaces and courtyards.

11.1b Why Induction Cooking Is NOT in the Evening Battery Budget
A critical design clarification before reading the BESS sizing: the overnight battery allowance of 1.8 kWh per household is sized for BASE LOADS ONLY — lighting, electronics, refrigeration, fans, and television. It does not include cooking loads. This is deliberate and essential.
The arithmetic of evening cooking: 2,000 households × 1,800W induction hob × 1 hour cooking = 3,600 kWh drawn in a single evening hour. At 7pm, with zero solar generation, this must come entirely from BESS. Providing this from battery storage would require 3,600 kWh additional BESS capacity — roughly 18 additional CATL EnerOne units at KES 100M more. The project cost would increase by KES 100M to solve a problem that SACCO LPG already solves at zero capital cost.
The correct architecture: LPG handles thermal loads (cooking, water heating backup). Solar handles electrical loads (lighting, devices, refrigeration). BESS bridges the overnight electrical gap. This three-way separation — LPG for thermal, solar for electrical, BESS for overnight — is the defining cost-reduction insight of the Angawatch energy design. Any system that tries to use batteries for cooking is over-engineered and over-priced for a housing affordability project.
Phase 3 solar induction cooking (Year 10+): When the community has 5+ years of carbon credit income and the BESS has been optimised, individual household induction cooking can be absorbed by the growing solar surplus during the 10am–2pm peak window. A family’s 1.2 kWh/day induction cooking load (scheduled by the M-Pesa SmartMeter IoT system to solar peak hours) replaces the LPG cylinder entirely. But this transition only makes economic sense when solar surplus is confirmed growing — not in Year 1 when every kWh of surplus has revenue value as a grid export.
BESS BASE LOAD DESIGN: The 1.8 kWh/household overnight allowance covers: LED lights (0.18 kWh), ceiling fan (0.20 kWh), refrigerator (0.43 kWh), TV (0.15 kWh), phone charging (0.04 kWh), WiFi router (0.24 kWh), and miscellaneous electronics (0.55 kWh). Total: 1.79 kWh ≈ 1.8 kWh. LPG covers: all cooking (2-burner hob, 1.2 kWh equivalent thermal per day). Solar thermal covers: all hot water. This three-way split is the honest design.

IRRIGATION SYSTEM DESIGN: The Yatta Plateau slopes uphill from the canal — the site sits above the canal, not below it. This means water cannot flow from the canal to the farm by gravity. Instead, a 10kW solar high-lift pump at the canal boundary pumps water uphill during the wet seasons to fill the 15,000m³ retention pond at the farm’s highest elevation. The engineering logic: pump fills upward once per season, gravity distributes continuously for free. This is the correct physical model for an upslope site. The ‘gravity fill’ narrative used in earlier versions of this document was physically impossible given the site topography and has been corrected.

13.4 Sewage Treatment Chain — Source Separation Model
CRITICAL ENGINEERING CLARIFICATION: Blackwater (toilet waste) and greywater (sinks/showers) are kept COMPLETELY SEPARATE from the point of generation. Blackwater goes directly to the centralised biogas plant — it never returns to the residential cluster area. Only greywater is handled within the Ekeja cluster courtyard reed beds, because greywater is low-strength, low-pathogen wastewater that is safe and appropriate for proximity to residential areas.
STREAM 1 — Blackwater Treatment (toilet waste) — Centralised, Remote
Step B1 — Source separation at WC: Blackwater collected in dedicated HDPE gravity sewers (separate from greywater drain). No shared pipework between streams.
Step B2 — Biogas digesters (primary treatment): Raw blackwater → 6 × 100m³ Deenbandhu fixed-dome digesters. 25-day retention. Anaerobic digestion destroys 90%+ of pathogens. Produces biogas (fuel + electricity) + digestate.
Step B3 — Digestate liquid effluent: Liquid fraction (~1,080m³/day after digestion) is piped by gravity to the REMOTE constructed wetland at the YattaFarm boundary — minimum 200m from the nearest residential block. Never returns to cluster courtyard area.
Step B4 — Remote constructed wetland (YattaFarm boundary): Large-scale Phragmites australis (common reed) beds. 48-hour hydraulic retention. 85% BOD reduction. Located downwind and downslope from housing. Maintenance access by farm staff, not residents.
Step B5 — Slow sand filter + irrigation pond: Wetland effluent → 500mm sand filter → 5,000m³ irrigation pond → YattaFarm drip irrigation for non-food-contact crops only (chrysanthemums, fuel crops). NEVER for vegetable or food crop irrigation.
STREAM 2 — Greywater Treatment (sinks/showers) — Within Ekeja Courtyard
Step G1 — Courtyard reed bed per Ekeja cluster: Sink and shower greywater from each cluster (low-strength, low-pathogen) feeds a small constructed reed bed in the courtyard garden area. 24-hour retention. 70% BOD reduction.
Step G2 — Greywater recycled for toilet flushing: Reed bed outflow → communal storage cistern → toilet cisterns in the cluster. Replaces potable water for WC flushing entirely. Net saving: 96 litres/day per household.
Step G3 — WHY GREYWATER IN THE COURTYARD IS SAFE: Greywater has no faecal contamination, low pathogen load, and reed bed treatment makes it safe for proximity to residential areas. This is standard practice in East African eco-developments. Blackwater is NEVER routed through residential courtyard systems.
STREAM 3 — Potable Water: 50 LPCD Boreholes + Rainwater Top-Up
BASELINE: 3 × deep boreholes supply 400m³/day = 400,000 litres ÷ 8,090 residents = 49.4 litres per capita per day (effectively 50 LPCD) (LPCD). WHO minimum for basic services: 50 LPCD. For a middle-class community with full plumbing, this is tight — two teenagers showering for 10 minutes each would consume the household’s daily share. The solution: centralised rainwater harvesting on top of borehole supply.
Step P1 — Sources: (a) 3 × deep boreholes (Grundfos SP17-7, 5.5 kW each), pumped 8am–6pm solar. (b) Centralised rainwater: all apartment roofs + carport canopies guttered to centralised UV-filtered storage.
Step P2 — Defluoridation: Bone char filters at centralised water treatment plant before distribution (volcanic fluoride endemic in Yatta basaltic aquifer). Bone char produced from YattaFarm animal bones — closes another material loop.
Step P3 — Distribution: Elevated 200,000L storage tank gravity-feeds community potable supply. UV disinfection at tank outlet.
SUMMARY: Blackwater → remote biogas → remote wetland → farm. Greywater → courtyard reed bed → toilet flushing. No digestate or raw sewage ever enters the residential cluster area. This is the gold standard of source separation sanitation.
